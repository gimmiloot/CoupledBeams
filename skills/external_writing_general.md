# External writing general skill

This skill applies to any text not intended only for internal project use:
- journal articles;
- abstracts;
- introductions and literature reviews;
- reviewer responses;
- cover letters;
- emails to collaborators or editors.

## Core rule

Write for an outside reader, not for the repo.

## Do not leak internal project language

Do not use internal-note language in external prose, including expressions like:
- theorem-line;
- ready-to-use corollary;
- canonical package;
- proved_here;
- needs_caution;
- not_reached_yet;
- audit;
- ceiling diagnosis;
- branch-ready;
- migration log language.

Rewrite such material into ordinary academic prose.

## Claim discipline

Do not upgrade the status of statements.
Keep clear distinctions between:
- proved;
- assumed;
- observed numerically;
- suggested by figures;
- conjectured;
- left for future work.

## Style

External prose should be:
- clear;
- formal;
- logically progressive;
- terminologically stable;
- free of unnecessary jargon.

Prefer:
- one main claim per sentence;
- explicit transitions;
- ordinary academic wording.

Avoid:
- repo-oriented prose;
- unexplained shorthand;
- inflated generalities;
- abrupt logical jumps.

## Citation discipline

Every citation should have a visible function:
- general background;
- classical result;
- closely related model;
- immediate previous work;
- main comparison point.

Remove citations that no longer serve the rewritten text.

## Journal adaptation

When adapting a manuscript to a journal:
- follow the journal rules first;
- follow the template second;
- keep scientific meaning unchanged unless adaptation requires otherwise;
- do not preserve old formatting or bibliography machinery if it conflicts with the journal rules.
